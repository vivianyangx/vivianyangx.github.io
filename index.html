Does Raising Interest Rates Cause Loan Defaults? A Causal Inference Study

PythonPandasScikit-learnLogistic RegressionTwins MatchingECL ModellingCausal Inference

FairMoney, a Nigerian digital lending platform, needed to understand whether increasing loan interest rates actually causes borrowers to default — or whether defaults are driven entirely by the borrower's inherent risk profile. I owned the full data analysis pipeline and presentation delivery, applying three complementary methods across three customer segments (new, returning, top-up) to isolate the causal rate effect from confounders including risk decile, loan tenor, and loan amount.

Analytical Approach — 3 Methods
Exploratory Analysis
Baseline
Default rate by rate quintile × segment × risk decile to identify raw patterns before controlling for confounders.
Twins Matching
Quasi-causal
Paired loans with identical risk profile, segment, and tenor — compared default rates between high- and low-rate twins to isolate the rate effect.
Logistic Regression
Controlled
Segment-level regressions controlling for risk decile and tenor to estimate the marginal effect of rate on default probability.
Key Findings by Segment
+2.45pp
New customers — Lowest rate sensitivity. A +2.45pp default spread between high- and low-rate loans of the same risk profile. Default behaviour is driven primarily by the borrower's inherent risk, not by the interest rate charged. Rate increases are relatively safe for this segment.
+2.67pp
Returning customers — Moderate sensitivity. A +2.67pp default spread, with a logistic regression coefficient of +0.33 (vs +0.16 for new customers). Targeted rate adjustments can optimise revenue without significantly increasing default risk, but require careful calibration.
+11.73pp
Top-up customers — Most rate-sensitive segment by far. An +11.73pp default spread and a logistic regression coefficient of +0.65 — over 4× that of new customers. Value destruction is clear beyond the Q2 rate bucket. Recommendation: cap rate increases for this segment to protect portfolio quality.

Long/Short Hedge Fund Strategy: Exploiting Overconfidence in the AI Industry

PythonCRSP / WRDSCompustatEvent StudyLong/Short StrategyBehavioral FinanceFactor Modelling

Designed and backtested a market-neutral Long/Short equity strategy grounded in the behavioral finance concept of investor overconfidence. During AI industry booms, overconfident investors flood capital indiscriminately into all AI-related stocks, inflating weaker companies beyond their fundamental value. The strategy exploits this mispricing by going long on fundamentally strong AI leaders and short on sentiment-driven "hype" stocks — profiting from the divergence regardless of overall market direction.

Strategy Design
Long
High-quality AI leaders (NVDA, MSFT, GOOGL, META) — selected using a composite fundamental quality score updated quarterly: revenue growth YoY, gross margin, and EPS. Top 2 scorers held long each quarter.
Short
Hype-driven AI stocks (SOUN, BBAI, C3.ai, SERV) — companies riding the AI narrative without real business strength. Bottom 2 fundamental scorers shorted each quarter to exploit the eventual price correction.
No bias
Look-ahead bias eliminated — all fundamental scores computed using only data available at each quarterly earnings announcement date (rdq), ensuring the strategy is point-in-time accurate and implementable in practice.
Performance Results (2021–2025, CRSP Daily Data)
Cumulative Return
+10.9%
Strategy remained above breakeven throughout the entire sample period — consistent positive performance.
2022 Bear Market
−10% vs −23%
While the S&P 500 fell 23% in 2022, the strategy declined only 10% — demonstrating strong downside protection.
Annualised Return
+2.6%
Market-neutral strategy — not designed to beat the index in bull markets, but to generate consistent risk-adjusted returns with low market correlation.

Netflix Pricing Strategy: Does Raising Prices Hurt Subscriber Growth?

Python Panel Regression Fixed Effects Demand Elasticity Time-Series Analysis Regional Segmentation

Netflix has repeatedly raised subscription prices while global memberships continue to grow — but does monetisation actually come at the cost of subscriber growth? I analysed Netflix's quarterly regional data (Q1 2020–Q4 2024) across UCAN, EMEA, LATAM, and APAC to estimate demand elasticity and provide region-specific pricing recommendations for a streaming giant navigating the trade-off between short-term revenue and long-term growth.

Analytical Approach
Step 1
Baseline regression — estimated the relationship between ARM (Average Revenue per Membership) growth and subscriber growth using year-over-year changes, with region and year fixed effects to control for structural and macro differences across markets.
Step 2
Region-specific elasticity — extended the model with interaction terms to allow the ARM–subscriber relationship to vary by region, revealing that pricing sensitivity differs significantly across markets.
Step 3
Strategic implications — translated regression estimates into actionable pricing recommendations for each region, balancing revenue optimisation against subscriber growth risk.
Key Findings by Region

S-Mobile: Predicting Customer Churn with Machine Learning

Python Logistic Regression Random Forest Case Weights Gains Chart Churn Modelling

S-Mobile needed to identify which customers were at risk of churning before they left — enabling proactive retention outreach rather than expensive win-back campaigns. The core challenge: the training data had a 50% churn rate (oversampled for modelling), while the real-world churn rate is only ~2%. A model naively trained on this data would produce wildly miscalibrated predictions. I built and compared Logistic Regression and Random Forest models using case weights to ensure predictions were correctly scaled to the real population, then generated churn scores on the full representative sample to prioritise outreach.

Methodology
Step 1
Class imbalance correction — applied case weights (1 for churners, 49 for non-churners) so that the weighted training set reflects the true ~2% real-world churn rate, ensuring predicted probabilities are properly calibrated for business use.
Step 2
Model comparison — trained and evaluated both Weighted Logistic Regression and Random Forest on a held-out test set. Key predictors included retention calls, customer care contacts, overage charges, equipment age, and months on network.
Step 3
Gains chart evaluation — used gains charts to confirm both models substantially outperform random targeting. Selected the best model and applied it to the 1M-row representative sample to generate final churn probability scores for retention prioritisation.

Uplift Modelling — Measuring the True Incremental Effect of Advertising

PythonLogistic RegressionRandom ForestXGBoostUplift ModellingQini CurvesRCT Design

Built a two-model uplift framework for a gaming company (Creative Gaming) to measure the true incremental effect of advertising on customer conversion. The key distinction: standard conversion rate analysis tells you who converted — uplift modelling tells you who converted because of the ad, separating persuadable customers from those who would have converted anyway. This allows marketing spend to be directed where it actually creates value.

Methodology
Step 1
Randomised experiment (RCT) — used an existing A/B test where customers were randomly assigned to see or not see the ad, ensuring any difference in conversion is caused by the ad itself, not by pre-existing purchase intent.
Step 2
Two-model scoring — trained separate models on customers who saw the ad vs. those who did not, then compared their predicted conversion probabilities to calculate each customer's individual "lift score" — how much more likely they are to convert because of the ad.
Step 3
Targeting optimisation — ranked customers by lift score and used Qini curves to identify the sweet spot: the segment of "persuadable" customers where ad spend generates the highest incremental return, and beyond which spending more adds little value.
